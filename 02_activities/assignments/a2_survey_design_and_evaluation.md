## Part B - Survey Evaluation:

**Identify and describe survey features:**


1. Sample Type: This survey employed a probability stratified sampling design where the strata are province/census metropolitan areas in the ten provices of canada. A total of 27 strata spread across the ten provinces were included. Following stratification, a random sample of households was identified in each stratum and a single member of each household was surveyed. It should be noted that a minimum sample size was determined for each province that would produce a desired level of sampling variability across strata. Because of this, it could be argued that the sample design incorporated elements of non-probability quota sampling in addition to probability stratified sampling. Finally, this survey further included rejective sampling where sub-sampling was carried out on the designated "not volunteers" cohort. "Not volunteers" were randomly divided to a group that did a group that did a long interview and a group that did a short interview. In contrast, all designated "volunteers" did a long interview.

2. Sample Size: A total of 50,000 potential sample units (households) were contacted to participate in the survey. Of those, 40,000 potential sample units were sent the electronic survey. Of those, 24,000 were expected to complete the survey and contribute to the final sample size. So, the expected sample size was about ~24,000 individuals who responded to the survey. However, based on a response rate of 41.9%, this number would be closer to ~21,000 individuals.  

3. Target Population: The target population was all individuals 15 years of age and older living in the ten provinces of Canada excluding full-time (residing for more than six months) residents of institutions. **It should be noted that this is the target population stated by Statistics Canada which suggests that the three Canadian territories were excluded from the target population. However, archived information on past iterations of this survey indicate that the Canadian territories were included in the target population as of 2004. As such, the target population is unclear from the documentation.

4. Sampling Frame: The sampling frame includes a combination of land-line and cellular telephone numbers from individuals within the province/census metropolitan areas designed as strata in the sampling design. This information was accessed through census data and various administrative sources within Statistics Canada's dwelling frame. Phone numbers associated with the same address were grouped together to avoid soliciting responses from multiple members of the same household. 

5. Survey mode(s): The survey was administered to participants via an electronic questionnaire or through computer assisted telephone interviewing (i.e. via email or telephone).

6. Timeline: This survey is conducted every five years and was initiated in 1997. Surveys are administered and results are collected between September and December of the survey year and data references the twelve months proceeding the survey date.

7. Response rate: The overall response rate was 41.9%

8. Weights: During estimation, a basic estimation weighting factor was determined for analysis at the person level so that survey data was representative of the target population in regards to particular characteristics such as age and sex. As well, to adjust for rejective sampling of "not volunteers", the weight for "non-volunteers" that were not rejected (i.e. did the long interview) was multiplied by a specified factor. Finally, the estimation weights were further adjusted so that the income distribution reported in the survey data matched what was reported in the 2017 Canadian Income Survey (CIS). It should also be noted that bootstrap weights were  created when determining estimates of sampling variability.

9. Data Processing: Data processing utilized a generalized survey data processing system known as the Social Survey Processing Environment (SSPE) which is designed to ensure best practices and harmonized business processes are maintained during this step. During processing, a series of edits were performed both automatically (sometimes electronically while the survey was being administered) and manually on the survey data to verify that survey responses were internally consistent and to detect errors in responses such as out of range answers. Additionally, all interviewer comments were reviewed by the head office during editing to provide further context regarding potential errors that may require editing.  

10. Cleaning, imputation, etc: Imputation was completed on the survey data to fill in non-responses based on the experiences of other respondents that possessed similar or identical characteristics to the "non-responder". Here, a "donor" for a potential non-responder was identified using a score function where shared characteristics between donor and non-responder increased the donor score. The donor with the highest score ("the nearest donor") was determined and their answer(s) were imputed into the non-responder's survey. If more than one "nearest donor" was identified, a single donor was chosen at random. If this method of donor imputation could not be used, mean imputation from a pool of donors was utilized to fill in non-responses. The imputation process included nine-steps with non-responses being imputed in the following order: personal income and family income, formal volunteering variables, informal volunteering variables, donation variables and solicitation variables.

11. Sources of error: There are multiple potential sources of error in this survey. First, this survey is subject to sampling error where estimates will vary from sample to sample and overall sample estimates will differ from the target population value simply because the entire target population could not be surveyed. Next, this survey will be subject to various forms of non-sampling error including non-response error and coverage error. Non-response error describes error resulting from individuals not answering some part or all of the items on the survey. This type of error may occur at the household level where a survey response is not given for a chosen household and also at the individual level where a survey response was not provided by an individual. Weighting was used as a means to account for non-response error. Coverage error describes error resulting from the sampling frame not matching the target population. This survey likely suffers from undercoverage since households without telephones or for which phone numbers were not recorded in the materials used to create lists were not covered by the sampling frame. Additional non-sampling error may results from errors in responses given by survey participants and errors during data processing steps.

12. Limitations, known biases, etc: The sources of error discussed in the previous section will introduce bias into the survey results. Non-response error will potentially cause non-response bias in estimates if participants who completed the survey differed systematically from those who did not respond or provides some proportion of non-responses. As well, coverage error will potentially cause selection bias in the form of coverage bias where the exclusion of households not covered by the sampling frame results in sample estimations differing from the target population value. Statistics Canada suggest that the bias resulting from coverage error will be negligible since the proportion of the population excluded is likely small, but they cannot know this for certain. One further limitation that I noted in this survey is that appears to exclude the Canadian Territories from the target population entirely despite the presumed importance of these regions in understanding national metrics. It should be noted that archived documents suggest that the territories have been and are potentially included in this survey in some capacity, but there is not record of this in the 2018 survey documentation. Without information from the territories, results from this survey cannot be considered representative of the entirety of Canada and any federal policy changes based on them would be systematically biased against communities in the territories. As well, documentation related to the modifications that have been made to the survey since its inception suggest that changes have been so drastic such that results from subsequent surveys cannot be compareds. This is a major limitation as it prevents statisticians from obtaining longitudinal results about how patterns of volunteering in Canada have changed over time which is one of the main objectives of general social surveys like this one.
